About

In 2020, Valerie Stoehr began a series of works that use code—HTML and CSS, the language of the web—as tools to explore constructs of power, gender, and spirituality in code.

  • Stoehr's first series, Beasts and Serpants, reimagines a child's toy in digital form. The work is based on the traditional toy children can manipulate to display an imagined creatures from a combination of interchangeable heads, torsos, and legs from disparate animals. The pieces consider the nature and purpose of play and proposes a new narrative of play in a digital landscape. Just as children use toys to make sense of and assign meaning to the world, this project articulates a new narrative about gender, violence, and supposed best practices for digital gaming.

  • Stoehr's second series, Samplers, juxtaposes hand-worked needlework samplers with a coding alphabet and the concept of semantic structure. In traditional stitched samplers, each letter of the alphabet is often portayed in a matrix, and additional symbols or representations of landscape of culture may appear. Stoehr's digital tapestries similarly expose the function and raw beauty of code and information architecture among elements of a digital landscape.

  • The third series, In The Meetinghouse, references Stoehr's Quaker experience. Digitals structures defined by code suggest the physical configuration in which Quakers assemble to meet for worship. Within those defined structures, objects transform over time and in relationship to each other. The work reflects on the fluid, multidimensional, and ultimately experiential component of a Quaker gathering for worship.

  • In Wadena County, in contrast to earlier explorations of play, Stoehr uses sparse code structures to consider the nature of work. With a restrained digital vocabulary and an explosion of matricies, Stoehr uses pattern and repetition to propose a digital statement of midwestern aesthetics, experience, and culture. The work is based on the barn beam from Stoehr's grandparent's Sebeka, Minnesota, farm.

Stoehr earned a women's studies degree from the University of Minnesota. In 1985 she moved to Oakland, CA, and began a career in the tech industry, documenting and designing emerging technologies. She currently resides in Afton, MN.
