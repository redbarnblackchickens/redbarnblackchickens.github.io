About

In 2020, Valerie Stoehr began a series of works that use code—HTML and CSS, the language of the web—as tools to explore constructs of power, gender, and spirituality in code.

  • Stoehr's first series, Beasts and Serpents, reimagines a child's toy, and the role of toys in making sense and meaning from a child's experience, in digital form. The work is based on the traditional toy that children manipulate to display an imaginary creatures from interchangeable images of the upper, middle, and lower parts of animals.

  • Stoehr's second series, Samplers, juxtaposes hand-worked needlework samplers with a coding alphabet and the concept of semantic structure. In traditional stitched samplers, each letter of the alphabet is often portayed in a matrix, and additional symbols or representations of landscape of culture may appear. Stoehr's digital tapestries similarly expose the function and raw beauty of code and information architecture among elements of a digital landscape.

  • The third series, In The Meetinghouse, references Stoehr's Quaker experience. Digital structures defined by code suggest the physical configuration in which Quakers assemble to meet for worship. Within those defined structures, objects transform over time and in relationship to each other. The work reflects on the fluid, multidimensional, and ultimately experiential component of a Quaker gathering for worship.

  • In Olmsted County, in contrast to earlier explorations of play, Stoehr uses sparse code structures to consider the nature of work. With a restrained digital vocabulary and an explosion of matricies, Stoehr uses pattern and repetition to propose a digital statement of midwestern aesthetics, experience, and culture. The work is based on the barn beam from Stoehr's grandparent's homestead in Sebeka, Minnesota.

  • The Wadena County series marks Stoehr's turn to pairing physical objects with her digital pieces. The first piece in the series is based on a barn beam from Stoehr's grandparent's homestead in Sebeka, Minnesota. The beam emerges vertically from a base. Smaller objects from the farmstead rise above the beam; their relative sizes and position suggest relationships to each other and to the supporting beam below. The pieces in this project explore and sometimes subvert the rules of information design in the physical and the digital world.

Stoehr earned a women's studies degree from the University of Minnesota. In 1985 she moved to Oakland, CA, and began a career in the tech industry, documenting and designing emerging technologies. She currently resides in Afton, MN.
